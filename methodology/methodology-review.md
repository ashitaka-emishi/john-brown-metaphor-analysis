# Methodology Paper Review

Date: 2026-07-16

Goal: `goals/methodology/M40-review-methodology.md`

Artifact reviewed: `methodology-paper/methodology-paper.qmd`

## Result

The methodology paper survives adversarial review only as a modest synthesis
and case-study report. It should not claim to invent a wholly new research
form, validate a method universally, or solve the epistemic risks of
AI-assisted interpretation.

## Critique Decisions

### The method is only a research compendium with new terminology

Decision: partially accepted.

The paper already situates the method in reproducible research and research
compendia. The draft should more explicitly state that the contribution is a
governance adaptation for interpretive scholarship, not a new genus of research
object.

### The artifact model falsely mechanizes interpretation

Decision: partially accepted.

The paper says interpretation cannot be mechanical, but the artifact-chain
language could still imply clean transformation from sources to claims. The
artifact chain should be framed as an audit device, not a truth procedure.

### Human gates are ceremonial rather than effective

Decision: partially accepted.

The case records many human approvals, but all interventions are accepted. That
weakens evidence that gates resist AI proposals. The gates were still effective
in specific moments because work stopped at corpus, thesis, publication, and
findings gates.

### The case study is biased because the method was developed during the case

Decision: accepted.

This is a developmental case study, not an independent validation. The workflow
that generated the historical paper also generated the methodology being
evaluated.

### Process logging altered the research process

Decision: accepted.

Prospective capture likely changed behavior by making decisions more visible
and increasing attention to auditable artifacts.

### One case provides insufficient evidence

Decision: accepted.

The conclusion should avoid any implication that the method is validated beyond
the Lincoln case.

### Documentation cost exceeds its scholarly value

Decision: partially accepted.

In this case, documentation caught errors and clarified decisions. In a smaller
or less risky project, the cost could exceed value.

### AI disclosure remains inadequate

Decision: accepted.

The paper cites AI authorship/accountability guidance but needs a fuller
disclosure model: what AI did, what it did not decide, and where human approval
entered.

### Reproducibility is confused with validity

Decision: rejected with caution.

The draft repeatedly distinguishes validation from interpretive truth. The
conclusion should nevertheless preserve reproducibility as an audit condition,
not a validity claim.

## Required Revisions Completed

- Narrowed originality claims.
- Added a proportionality principle for documentation burden.
- Added explicit developmental-case-study bias.
- Added observer-effect treatment.
- Added AI disclosure expectations.
- Strengthened language that artifacts audit interpretation rather than
  mechanizing it.
- Strengthened conclusion around reproducibility versus validity.
