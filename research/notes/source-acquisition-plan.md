# Source Acquisition Plan

## Status

This is a proposed plan for corpus approval. It does not authorize acquisition beyond reversible discovery and does not mark any source verified.

## Priorities

1. Establish the Gettysburg textual corpus.
2. Establish the ceremonial context.
3. Establish the Lincoln comparative corpus.
4. Establish a limited reception sample.
5. Obtain secondary scholarship through lawful access.

## Phase 1: Gettysburg Textual Corpus

Acquire authoritative records for the five Gettysburg Address copies:

- Nicolay Copy.
- Hay Copy.
- Everett Copy.
- Bancroft Copy.
- Bliss Copy.

Preferred repository: Library of Congress. Each manuscript copy needs a separate raw file, normalized text file, source-register row, hash, and variant note. Claims about the delivered address must remain provisional until manuscript status and variant evidence are inspected.

Risks:

- The delivered wording is not identical to every later copy.
- "Under God" and other variants require copy-specific treatment.
- Repository exhibit pages may not be sufficient as canonical item records.

## Phase 2: Ceremony and Context

Acquire or identify:

- Edward Everett's oration.
- David Wills's invitation to Lincoln.
- The official dedication program.
- Prayers, hymns, and other ceremonial materials.
- Lincoln's November 20, 1863 letter to Everett.

Preferred repositories: Library of Congress, National Archives, HathiTrust public-domain scans, Internet Archive public-domain scans, university special collections, or authoritative documentary editions.

Risks:

- The official program, prayers, and hymns are unresolved identities in the current register.
- A modern web transcription of Everett's oration may not be the best evidence.
- Ceremonial materials may have multiple printings or later reproductions.

## Phase 3: Comparative Lincoln Corpus

Acquire authoritative texts for the proposed Lincoln sources:

- Lyceum Address, January 27, 1838.
- House Divided Speech, June 16, 1858.
- Cooper Union Address, February 27, 1860.
- First Inaugural Address, March 4, 1861.
- Proclamation Calling Forth the Militia, April 15, 1861.
- Special Session Message to Congress, July 4, 1861.
- First Annual Message, December 3, 1861.
- Meditation on the Divine Will, probably 1862.
- Second Annual Message, December 1, 1862.
- Emancipation Proclamation, January 1, 1863.
- Letter to James C. Conkling, August 26, 1863.
- Third Annual Message, December 8, 1863.
- Letter to Albert G. Hodges, April 4, 1864.
- Fourth Annual Message, December 6, 1864.
- Second Inaugural Address, March 4, 1865.
- Last Public Address, April 11, 1865.

Preferred repositories: National Archives, Library of Congress, American Presidency Project for presidential messages where no stronger local edition is selected, National Park Service for memorial text discovery, and authoritative collected editions where available.

Risks:

- Some online transcriptions omit textual apparatus.
- Private or semi-private texts require careful public/private classification.
- Public presidential documents and letters have different rhetorical situations.

## Phase 4: Reception Sample

Build a small, documented reception sample before any broad claims about contemporary response:

- Republican/pro-administration praise or republication.
- Democratic/opposition criticism.
- Harrisburg `Patriot and Union` editorial named in the dossier.
- Reports preserving variant wording.
- Everett's response to Lincoln.

Preferred repositories: Chronicling America, state newspaper archives with public access, Library of Congress guides, university exhibits only as discovery aids, and preserved newspaper scans when legally accessible.

Risks:

- Partisan alignment and issue dates may be uncertain.
- OCR errors are likely and must be checked against page images.
- Later summaries cannot replace issue-level evidence.

## Phase 5: Secondary Scholarship

Obtain the named secondary works through library, purchase, existing lawful copies, or controlled previews sufficient for bibliographic orientation:

- Boritt, `The Gettysburg Gospel`.
- Conant, ed., `The Gettysburg Address`.
- Guelzo, `Gettysburg: The Last Invasion`.
- Peterson, `Lincoln in American Memory`.
- Schwartz, `Abraham Lincoln and the Forge of National Memory`.
- White, `Lincoln's Greatest Speech`.
- Wills, `Lincoln at Gettysburg`.
- Zarefsky, `Lincoln, Douglas, and Slavery`.

Risks:

- These works are copyrighted. Do not bulk download or reproduce them.
- Access may depend on the scholar's library holdings.
- Secondary claims must be cited and separated from primary evidence.

## Network and Provenance Rules

Any network acquisition tool must include dry-run behavior, timeout, conservative rate limiting, descriptive user agent, and explicit output paths. Acquisition must respect access controls, robots.txt, terms, and copyright.

For each acquired item, record:

- repository and stable URL or bibliographic locator;
- access date;
- rights status;
- acquisition method;
- local raw path;
- SHA-256 hash;
- normalization path if created;
- unresolved provenance issues.

## Proposed Corpus for Human Approval

Core corpus:

- The five Gettysburg Address copies.
- Everett's oration.
- Wills's invitation.
- Dedication program, prayers, hymns, and associated ceremonial materials once identified.
- Lincoln's November 20, 1863 letter to Everett.

Comparative Lincoln corpus:

- The sixteen Lincoln writings listed in Phase 3.

Reception corpus:

- A limited November-December 1863 sample balanced by political orientation and source type, including the Harrisburg `Patriot and Union`.

Secondary corpus:

- The eight named secondary works, used for interpretive mapping and counterarguments.

Proposed exclusions for now:

- Large-scale soldier correspondence.
- Later commemorations beyond source-discovery needs.
- General war theory and sacrifice theory not directly tied to the approved corpus.

This is the corpus approval human gate.
