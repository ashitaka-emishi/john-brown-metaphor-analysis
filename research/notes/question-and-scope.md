# Question and Scope

## Research Question

How does the Gettysburg Address transform the deaths of soldiers into a sacred obligation to preserve and refound the nation, and what does this reveal about Lincoln's understanding of the nature of war?

This question remains provisional until the corpus approval gate. The dossier supplies hypotheses and proposed sources, not verified evidence.

## Unit of Analysis

The primary unit of analysis is a passage: a complete sentence, clause group, or rhetorically coherent phrase in which Lincoln or a contextual source frames war, death, sacrifice, obligation, national survival, freedom, providence, judgment, or democratic government.

The project will distinguish four levels of claim:

1. Source text: exact wording verified against a preserved local source.
2. Observation: description of language, structure, genre, provenance, or variant wording.
3. Inference: interpretive explanation of what the observed language does rhetorically or politically.
4. Conclusion: a broader claim about Lincoln's public rhetoric, wartime thought, or the nature of war.

No conclusion may be treated as established unless it is mapped through the evidence matrix and claims file.

## Temporal Scope

The focal event is the Gettysburg cemetery dedication on November 19, 1863. The comparative Lincoln corpus runs from the Lyceum Address of January 27, 1838, through the Last Public Address of April 11, 1865.

The primary interpretive window is the Civil War presidency, March 4, 1861, through April 11, 1865. Earlier sources may show antecedent concepts, but they will not be treated as direct evidence of Lincoln's 1863 wartime meaning without a stated inference.

Reception evidence is initially limited to contemporary responses from November and December 1863 unless later goals expand the memory-history dimension.

## Public Rhetoric and Private Belief

The Gettysburg Address, inaugural addresses, annual messages, proclamations, public letters, and public speeches are evidence of public rhetoric and official self-presentation. They may support claims about how Lincoln publicly constructed the war, but they do not by themselves prove private belief.

Private memoranda or letters may be used to test whether public claims align with or diverge from private reflection. Even then, the project will avoid unsupported psychological attribution. Claims about Lincoln's belief must name the evidentiary basis and confidence level.

## Civil War and All War

The project studies Lincoln's interpretation of the American Civil War. It will not infer that Lincoln believed all wars are sacred, regenerative, sacrificial, or politically redemptive unless evidence explicitly supports that broader claim.

The Gettysburg Address may reveal a theory of this war as a test of democratic government, a crisis of national survival, and a possible refounding through freedom. Any statement about war in general must be marked as comparative inference and tested against contrary evidence.

## Operational Definition of Sacrifice

The project will not code a death as sacrifice merely because a source mentions death, casualties, bravery, mourning, or military service.

A passage may be coded as sacrifice only when death or mortal risk is joined to at least one of these features:

1. Voluntary giving or self-offering.
2. Exchange for a collective good, such as national life, freedom, or democratic survival.
3. Consecration, hallowing, sanctification, or comparable sacred transformation.
4. A debt, duty, or obligation imposed upon survivors.
5. Collective benefit or representative meaning assigned to the dead.

The coding record must identify the textual basis, alternative readings, and confidence. Possible alternatives include conventional cemetery rhetoric, republican civic language, Christian memorial vocabulary, patriotic mobilization, or later canonical memory.

## Initial Boundaries

Included:

- The five Gettysburg Address manuscript copies proposed in the dossier.
- Everett's oration, Wills's invitation, the official program, prayers, hymns, and related ceremonial materials when identified.
- Lincoln's proposed comparative writings from 1838 to 1865.
- Contemporary reception evidence sufficient to test whether the address was immediately understood as sacred, political, partisan, ordinary, or otherwise contested.
- Secondary scholarship needed to map major interpretive schools and counterarguments.

Excluded unless later approved:

- Large-scale soldier diary or letter corpora.
- Post-1863 commemorative memory beyond narrowly necessary reception/context checks.
- General theories of war, nationalism, sacrifice, or political theology not anchored in the approved Lincoln/Gettysburg corpus.
- Paywalled or restricted sources that cannot be accessed lawfully.
