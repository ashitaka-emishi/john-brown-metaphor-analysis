# Provisional Thesis

Date: 2026-07-16

Status: approved by scholar on 2026-07-16.

## Approved Thesis

In the Gettysburg Address, Lincoln transforms Union soldiers' deaths into a
public obligation by placing them inside a sequence of founding, testing,
consecration, dedication, and rebirth. The nation is presented as an inherited
proposition dedicated to equality; the Civil War tests whether such a nation can
endure; the dead have given life to the nation and consecrated the battlefield;
therefore the living must take increased devotion to the unfinished work so
that popular government receives a new birth of freedom and does not perish.

This reveals Lincoln's understanding of the Civil War at Gettysburg as more
than combat or policy enforcement: it is a crisis in which battlefield death can
be made to impose political and moral duty on the living. But the evidence does
not support reducing Lincoln's whole understanding of war to sacrifice. Across
the comparative corpus, Lincoln also treats the war as constitutional
preservation, democratic test, emancipation policy, providential uncertainty,
judgment over slavery, and practical reconstruction. Gettysburg is therefore
best read as a concentrated public ceremonial construction of this war, not as a
complete private or universal theory of war.

## Distinctions Required In The Paper

- Public rhetoric, not private belief.
- This Civil War, not war in general.
- Rhetorical transformation of death, not denial of bodily violence.
- Sacred register, not necessarily settled theology.
- Emancipation-compatible rebirth, not a full policy statement in the Address
  alone.
- Lincoln's Gettysburg construction, not Lincoln's entire war philosophy.

## Proposed Revisions From Dossier Thesis

The dossier thesis should be narrowed in four ways:

1. Replace any claim that Lincoln presents war simply as sacrifice with the
   claim that Gettysburg constructs battlefield death as obligation.
2. State that Everett and cemetery convention share sacrifice and consecration
   language, so Lincoln's distinctiveness lies in compression and sequence.
3. Treat `under God` and other sacred phrases through manuscript variants.
4. Add comparative limits: constitutional necessity, emancipation policy,
   providential uncertainty, judgment, and reconstruction.
