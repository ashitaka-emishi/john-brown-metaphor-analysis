# Goal 20 Coding Memo

Date: 2026-07-16

## Scope

This memo records the evidence-coding pass for Goal 20. It uses the
verified or qualified corpus preserved during Goal 10 and does not treat the
provisional dossier as evidence.

The pass prioritizes:

- the five Gettysburg Address manuscript witnesses;
- Wills's ceremonial invitation;
- Everett's dedication oration and the cemetery-pamphlet context;
- comparative Lincoln texts from the approved corpus;
- a limited reception sample, including the derivative Dickinson transcript for
  `SRC-0011` with its original-image caveat.

## Codebook Use

Rows in `research/data/evidence-matrix.csv` follow the project schema:

- `passage` records quotation or faithful source text;
- `observation` states what the passage visibly does;
- `interpretation` states the analytic inference;
- `alternative_reading` records a serious non-thesis reading;
- `disconfirming` marks evidence that complicates or limits the working thesis.

Sacrifice is not coded from death alone. It is coded only when death is joined
to giving, exchange, consecration, collective benefit, or obligation.

## Findings

The Bliss Copy provides the cleanest working form for close reading because it
contains the familiar final sequence: `gave their lives`, `consecrated`,
`under God`, `new birth of freedom`, and `government of the people`.
Quotation-level use still requires checking the manuscript image against the
human transcription.

The Nicolay and Hay witnesses support the main structure but introduce important
variant cautions. Nicolay uses `died here` and `hallowed`; Hay uses `gave their
lives` and `consecrated`; both lack `under God`. Later Everett and Bancroft
copies include `under God`. Claims about sacred register must therefore be
variant-aware.

Everett shares sacrifice and consecration language, including the idea that men
`sacrifice their lives` so others may live safely and honorably. His oration,
however, gives more space to classical analogy, military necessity, invasion,
and political indictment. This comparison prevents overstating Lincoln's
uniqueness while clarifying Lincoln's compression.

The cemetery-pamphlet account of exposed and partly unburied remains supplies a
body-focused counterweight to Lincoln's euphemized diction. The Address makes
death meaningful but does not describe bodily destruction.

Comparative Lincoln texts show continuity and limits. The Lyceum, House
Divided, Cooper Union, First Inaugural, Militia Proclamation, First Annual
Message, and July 4, 1861 message establish inherited republican duty,
constitutional preservation, slavery crisis, and democratic self-government as
pre-Gettysburg frames. The Second Annual Message, Emancipation Proclamation,
Conkling letter, Third Annual Message, Hodges letter, Fourth Annual Message,
Second Inaugural, and Last Public Address add emancipation, Black military
service, reconstruction, providence, judgment, and constrained agency.

Reception evidence remains limited. The Dickinson derivative transcription of
the Harrisburg `Patriot and Union` editorial is useful for working analysis
because it records a hostile reading of the ceremony as partisan misuse of
sacred ground. It is not final quotation evidence until the original newspaper
image is acquired.

## Variant Comparison

The central movement survives across witnesses:

1. founding/dedication;
2. war as test;
3. battlefield/cemetery dedication;
4. soldier action as prior consecration or hallowing;
5. living dedication to unfinished work;
6. final resolution that the dead not die in vain.

The main coded variations are:

- `died here` in Nicolay versus `gave their lives` in Hay/later witnesses;
- `hallowed it` in Nicolay versus `consecrated it` in Hay/later witnesses;
- absence of `under God` in Nicolay and Hay;
- presence of `under God` in Everett, Bancroft, and Bliss witnesses.

These variants do not defeat the obligation thesis, but they lower confidence
for any claim that depends on a single sacred phrase.
