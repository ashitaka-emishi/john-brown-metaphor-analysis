# Theological-Language Review

Date: 2026-07-16

Artifact reviewed: `paper/paper.qmd`

## Result

The draft handles sacred language with appropriate caution. The main remaining
need is to prevent sacred rhetoric from being read as direct evidence of
Lincoln's private belief.

## Critique Decisions

### Rhetoric is mistaken for private belief

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: the draft repeatedly states that Gettysburg is public rhetoric, not
private confession. It also distinguishes textual variants, especially the
presence or absence of "under God" across witnesses.

Problem: the conclusion's phrasing, "Lincoln understood the Civil War," could
invite a private-belief reading.

Revision required: change the conclusion to say Lincoln publicly presented the
war in this way at Gettysburg.

### The Second Inaugural's judgment frame is understated

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: the draft gives the Second Inaugural a full counterevidence role and
identifies it as a different theological register: providence, slavery, and
judgment rather than Gettysburg's dedication and rebirth.

Problem: the conclusion should reinforce that the Second Inaugural is not
merely a supplement to Gettysburg but a distinct war frame.

Revision required: revise the conclusion's final paragraph to preserve the
Second Inaugural's independent judgment frame.

### Sacred language is over-Christianized

Decision: rejected.

Evidence: the paper does not build its argument on denominational doctrine,
atonement theology, or a claim about Lincoln's churchly orthodoxy. It treats
consecration, hallowing, dedication, devotion, and "under God" as public sacred
language with textual variation.

Recommended caution: remove "political theology" from the keyword list because
the draft does not develop that technical frame.

## Remaining Theological Limits

- The Bliss Copy's "under God" wording remains variant-aware evidence, not a
  license to harmonize all manuscripts.
- Comparative theological claims should remain anchored in the Meditation on
  the Divine Will and Second Inaugural, not inferred from Gettysburg alone.
