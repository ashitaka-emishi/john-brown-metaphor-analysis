# Anachronism Review

Date: 2026-07-16

Artifact reviewed: `paper/paper.qmd`

## Result

The draft mostly avoids anachronism by distinguishing Lincoln's terms from the
paper's analytic vocabulary. Two phrases need tightening: "refound" and
"political theology."

## Critique Decisions

### "Refound" imports later theoretical vocabulary

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: Lincoln says "new birth of freedom," not "refounding." The paper's
claim that preservation becomes transformation is supported by the Address and
comparative emancipation/reconstruction evidence, but "refound" is analytic
shorthand.

Revision required: replace summary-level "refound" language with "new birth of
freedom" or "transform" unless the paper explicitly marks refounding as an
analytic term.

### "Political theology" overstates the frame

Decision: accepted.

Evidence: the draft analyzes public sacred language and providential/judgment
registers, but it does not develop a formal political-theology framework.

Revision required: remove "political theology" from the keyword list.

### Sacrifice imports later civil-religion language

Decision: rejected with caution.

Evidence: sacrifice language appears in the contemporaneous Everett pamphlet,
and the draft defines sacrifice by textual features in the Address rather than
by later civil-religion theory. The claim is about rhetoric and public meaning,
not an assumption that nineteenth-century soldiers all understood themselves
through later memorial culture.

Recommended caution: continue treating sacrifice as constructed by the speech's
sequence rather than as a background essence of war death.

## Remaining Anachronism Limits

- Avoid using "nature of war" as if Lincoln offered a timeless theory.
- Keep emancipation and reconstruction claims comparative, since Gettysburg
  alone does not supply detailed policy content.
