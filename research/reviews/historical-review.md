# Historical Review

Date: 2026-07-16

Artifact reviewed: `paper/paper.qmd`

## Result

The draft is historically usable after narrowing. It does not need a new thesis,
but several phrasings should more clearly distinguish Gettysburg's public
ceremonial construction from Lincoln's whole war thought.

## Critique Decisions

### The Civil War is generalized into war as such

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: the introduction already says the phrase "nature of war" must be
disciplined and limits the paper to the Civil War as publicly constructed at
Gettysburg. The counterevidence section also treats the Second Inaugural,
Hodges letter, Emancipation Proclamation, and Last Public Address as plural
war frames.

Problem: the conclusion still asks what Gettysburg reveals about Lincoln's
"understanding of the nature of war" and says "Lincoln understood the Civil
War." That wording risks implying direct access to Lincoln's private belief or
a general theory.

Revision required: revise the conclusion to say that Gettysburg reveals how
Lincoln publicly presented this Civil War in this ceremonial setting.

### Soldier agency is ignored

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: the draft already rejects any claim that every soldier consciously
offered himself as a sacrifice. It says the Address does not recover the dead
soldiers' intentions and that "gave their lives" generalizes across many
different deaths.

Problem: the paper should also say positively that the speech attributes
consecrating power to soldier action and struggle, not merely to passive death.

Revision required: add a sentence preserving soldier agency as action while
still avoiding unsupported claims about individual motive.

### Omission of bodily violence is called intentional concealment

Decision: partially accepted.

Evidence: the draft uses the cemetery pamphlet's description of exposed and
partly buried remains as a check on Gettysburg's elevated rhetoric. It also
says the speech does not describe battle or dead bodies.

Problem: one sentence says Gettysburg "almost entirely suppresses" legal
machinery, strategy, emancipation policy, and divine judgment. "Suppresses" can
sound intentional and can bleed into the bodily-violence issue.

Revision required: replace intentional-sounding language with a descriptive
claim that those frames are largely absent from the Address.
