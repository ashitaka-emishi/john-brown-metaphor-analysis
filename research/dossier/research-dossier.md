# John Brown Metaphor Analysis Research Dossier

Status: provisional research specification, not verified evidence.

## Primary Research Question

How did John Brown use metaphor, biblical allusion, and religious typology in
his surviving writings and authenticated statements to understand his
antislavery mission, his use of violence, the failure at Harpers Ferry, and his
approaching death?

## Compact Research Question

How did John Brown's symbolic language construct his understanding of his
actions and death in the struggle against slavery?

## Significance

This project examines Brown's symbolic language as part of his own
self-representation rather than as a shortcut to claims about his private mind.
The study matters because Brown's antislavery violence and execution were
quickly absorbed into later martyr, fanatic, criminal, prophetic, military, and
national-memory frames. A source-grounded study must first ask what Brown's
surviving writings and authenticated statements actually permit us to say.

## Unit of Analysis

The basic unit is a locatable passage from a preserved or well-attested source.
Each passage may support one or more observations about metaphor, biblical
quotation, biblical allusion, religious typology, providential claim, moral
analogy, role construction, violence, failure, or death.

## Chronological Boundaries

The proposed chronological scope extends from Brown's early surviving
correspondence through his execution on December 2, 1859. Later reception may
be consulted to identify distortions or afterlives, but it is outside the
primary evidentiary question unless explicitly marked as reception history.

## Source-Type Distinctions

- Private letters: evidence for how Brown represented matters to specific
  recipients, not automatic access to hidden motive.
- Public statements: evidence for public self-presentation and argument.
- Reported statements: usable only with provenance, witness, genre, and
  transmission caveats.
- Later recollections: contextual or discovery evidence unless the scholar
  approves a stronger use with explicit limits.

## Initial Corpus Periods

1. Formation and baseline: early correspondence through the beginning of the
   Kansas conflict.
2. Kansas and armed resistance: writings and authenticated statements around
   armed antislavery action.
3. Preparation for Harpers Ferry: planning correspondence and organizational
   documents.
4. Capture, trial, imprisonment, and execution: post-raid explanations, trial
   statements, prison correspondence, and final statements.

No period is complete until its proposed source set is reviewed and approved by
the scholar.

## Source Hierarchy

Tier 1 evidence includes surviving manuscripts, authenticated facsimiles,
signed letters or documents, archival transcriptions linked to preserved
manuscripts, court statements with strong textual provenance, and
contemporaneous documents whose authorship can be established.

Tier 2 evidence includes contemporary newspaper transcriptions, courtroom or
stenographic reports, immediately published interviews, documented duplicate
copies, and family or recipient transcriptions with known provenance.

Tier 3 evidence includes later recollection, memoir, partisan paraphrase,
unattributed quotation, quotation without identifiable manuscript or
contemporary witness, and secondary quotation without accessible source
verification. Tier 3 material cannot support a thesis-bearing quotation without
human approval and an explicit limitation.

## Initial Symbolic-Language Categories

These are discovery categories, not conclusions: bondage and liberation;
captivity, chains, bonds, deliverance, and rescue; divine cause and human
instrument; law, higher law, justice, guilt, sentence, and judgment; family,
brotherhood, children, and the despised poor; war, command, defense,
resistance, and invasion; blood, sacrifice, suffering, offering, and martyrdom;
seed, harvest, fruition, and future victory; providential time, testing,
discipline, and vindication; body versus cause; death versus continued moral
action; failure transformed into testimony; slavery as sin, crime, oppression,
disease, or captivity; nation, government, constitution, and moral order.

Categories may be revised, split, merged, or rejected after corpus review.

## Tentative Hypotheses

These hypotheses are provisional and must be tested against acquired sources:

1. Brown framed slavery through bondage, injustice, and moral obligation.
2. Brown represented himself as subordinate to a divine or moral cause rather
   than as an autonomous originator.
3. Military and rescue metaphors coexisted in tension.
4. Post-capture writings may transform tactical failure into testimony or
   providential efficacy.
5. Blood and death may become more prominent symbolic resources as execution
   approaches.
6. Brown's later language may have enabled later martyr construction without
   being identical to it.
7. Brown's symbolic system may prove coherent across decades, or it may contain
   discontinuities produced by changing audiences and circumstances.

## Disconfirming Possibilities

The project must test whether:

- Brown's prison language differs substantially from earlier correspondence.
- Sacrificial language intensified only after conviction.
- Brown remained primarily a practical military actor rather than a
  martyr-oriented actor.
- Later editors imposed a stronger Christological or prophetic frame than Brown
  used himself.
- Brown used multiple inconsistent symbolic frames.
- Biblical language reflected ordinary nineteenth-century Protestant idiom
  rather than a distinctive self-construction.
- Metaphor frequency does not correspond to interpretive importance.
- The surviving corpus is distorted by preservation and publication bias.
- Reported trial statements differ materially across witnesses.

## Source-Access and Copyright Risks

Likely risks include manuscript access restrictions, image-use limits,
copyright in modern editions, unstable newspaper OCR, derivative transcriptions
without page images, paywalled scholarship, conflicting transcriptions, and
uncertain attribution for reported speech. The project must not bypass access
controls or treat inaccessible modern editions as if they had been inspected.

## Limits on Inner Psychology

The project must not claim direct access to Brown's private mind. Use
formulations such as "Brown represented," "Brown framed," "surviving writings
suggest," "authenticated statements construct," and "Brown's language permits
the interpretation." Claims about private belief require explicit evidence and
human approval.

## Relationship to Later Reception

Later Northern, Southern, abolitionist, Confederate, memorial, literary, and
popular reception is outside the primary question. It may be used to identify
how later readers transformed Brown, but not as evidence of Brown's original
wording or self-understanding.
